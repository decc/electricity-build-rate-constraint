This scenario results in  renewable electricity in 2020

We assume that between now and 2020 GW/yr of renewable electricity capacity is built and of that capacity is wind. This leads to TWh of renewable electricity in 2020 (i.e.,) compared with our indicative target of 105 TWh (30%).

Additional low-carbon generation is delayed until

We assume that we want to delay construction of further low-carbon electricity until the last possible moment, but still decarbonise electricity by 2050 in order to meet our legally binding target. How late we can leave it depends on the fastest we can imagine building low-carbon electricity. In this case we we assume that, at most, GW of low carbon capacity can be built each year, and that the maximum rate at which the low carbon construction industry can expand is a year. This means that new low-carbon capacity doesn't need to start generating until in order for electricity to more-or-less decarbonise by 2050.

One critical assumption is that of the low-carbon capacity that is subsequently built is wind. This is important because wind delivers less energy per GW of capacity than nuclear, CCS or bioenergy.

We have also assumed that, after 2020, the rate of low-carbon construction falls by at most in any given year, and never falls below GW per year.

Warning: cannot get 2050 electricity emissions below 10 MtCO 2 given these assumptions. Try increasing the maximum low carbon build rate after 2020.

The emissions intensity of electricity in 2030 is gCO2/kWh

To work out the emissions intensity in 2030, we assume that GW of nuclear and CCS is built by 2020 and that the emissions factor for remaining fossil fuel generation is gCO2/kWh in 2020 and is expected to fall steadily towards gCO2/kWh by 2050.
